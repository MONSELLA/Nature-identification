BIG-5 PROJECT — NATURE DEFINITION (REFERENCE) — v2

DEFINITION

Nature includes:

- Non-human living entities: individual organisms, species, or groups of organisms
  — flora, fauna, or fungi — whether wild, semi-natural, domesticated, cultivated,
  or bred (e.g., a bird, a garden plant, a species of insect, cultivated mushrooms).
- Natural or semi-natural environments and ecosystems where such entities interact
  (e.g., a forest, a coral reef, a natural reserve, an urban park, a vegetable
  garden) — excluding purely administrative or political units (e.g., a country,
  county, or district), even when named after a natural feature.
- Non-living natural elements and processes, including:
  - Hydrological components (e.g., a river, pond, lake)
  - Atmospheric, meteorological, and seasonal components/processes (e.g., rain,
    wind, snow, fog, temperature, a season)
  - Geological and mineral components (e.g., rock, salt, oil)
  - Astrophysical elements and processes (e.g., sunset, sunrise, stars, moon,
    an eclipse, day/night)
  - Biophysical processes (e.g., photosynthesis, evapotranspiration, erosion)
- Activities that take place in or directly engage with any of the above (e.g.,
  hiking, surfing, fishing, gardening, birdwatching).
- Any representation of the above — whether a real, physically present instance,
  or a concept, symbol, or fictional/artistic depiction — that explicitly refers
  to or depicts one of the categories above. This includes stylized, imagined, or
  clearly non-literal depictions (e.g., a painting of a landscape, a cartoon
  animal, a video-game forest, a poem about the sea, a metaphor invoking nature,
  a virtual-reality scene) — these count as nature-relevant even though they are
  not a literal, unaltered depiction.

INCLUSION CRITERION

For real, physically present (unaltered) instances: an element counts as natural
if its alteration has not changed its material, structural, or functional
character from how it would appear in its natural habitat.
- Example: a live plant in a pot = natural. A wooden table whose surface no
  longer visibly resembles wood grain = not natural (too altered).
- Food and drink count as natural at any level of processing, as long as
  ingestion is the relevant framing (e.g., coffee, sugar, a meatball, pasta all
  count).

For symbolic, fictional, or artistic depictions: the alteration criterion above
does not apply. A stylized, imagined, or non-literal depiction still counts as
nature-relevant as long as it explicitly refers to or depicts one of the
categories in the DEFINITION above (e.g., a cartoon bird still counts as nature
even though it looks nothing like a real bird).

EXCLUSION CRITERION

An element only counts as nature if it explicitly and directly refers to or
depicts one of the categories above — not when it is used incidentally,
figuratively as an unrelated idiom, or purely as a unit of time/measurement with
no natural content in focus.
- Example: "summer school" = not nature (summer used only as a time reference).
  "hot summer" = nature (the season's natural/climatic character is in focus).
- Example: administrative or political units (e.g., a country, county, or
  district) are not nature, even if named after a natural feature. A natural
  reserve or urban park is nature.
