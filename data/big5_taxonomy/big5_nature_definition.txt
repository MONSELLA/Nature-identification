Nature includes:

- Non-human living entities: individual organisms, species, or groups of organisms — flora, fauna, or fungi — whether wild, semi-natural, domesticated, cultivated, or bred (e.g., a bird, a garden plant, a species of insect, cultivated mushrooms).
- Natural or semi-natural environments and ecosystems where such entities interact (e.g., a forest, a coral reef, a natural reserve, an urban park, a vegetable garden) — excluding purely administrative or political units (e.g., a country, county, or district), even when named after a natural feature.
- Non-living natural elements and processes, including:
  - Hydrological components (e.g., a river, pond, lake)
  - Atmospheric, meteorological, and seasonal components/processes (e.g., rain, wind, snow, fog, temperature, a season)
  - Geological and mineral components (e.g., rock, salt, oil)
  - Astrophysical elements and processes (e.g., sunset, sunrise, stars, moon, an eclipse, day/night)
  - Biophysical processes (e.g., photosynthesis, evapotranspiration, erosion)
- Processed Food & Drink (The Ingestion Criterion): Edible solid or liquid elements at any processing level count as nature. This explicitly includes processed foods and derivatives (e.g., pasta, a meatball, a tuna can, sugar, coffee, butter).
- Nature-Based Artefacts: Manufactured or crafted objects where the natural material of origin is clearly visually identifiable (e.g., a surface with visible wood grain, or distinct stone texture). This explicitly excludes artefacts with a homogenous, unidentifiable texture (e.g., smooth plastic, a painted statue).
- Activities that take place in or directly engage with any of the above (e.g., hiking, surfing, fishing, gardening, birdwatching).
- Any representation of the above — whether a real, physically present instance, or a concept, symbol, or fictional/artistic depiction — that explicitly refers to or depicts one of the categories above. This includes stylized, imagined, or clearly non-literal depictions (e.g., a painting of a landscape, a cartoon animal, a video-game forest, a poem about the sea, a metaphor invoking nature, a virtual-reality scene) — these count as nature-relevant even though they are not a literal, unaltered depiction.
